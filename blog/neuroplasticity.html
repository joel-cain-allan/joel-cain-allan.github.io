Neuroplasticity and the Growth Mindset

Thinkin' is linkin'

Neuroplasticity

Neuroplasticity is the ability of the brain to physically adapt as a result of the brain’s own efforts.

Understanding that the brain is capable of (and requires) altering its physical properties, is also an understanding that limitations are much less concrete than some pessimistic thought processes might suggest - working at something implies getting better at it, or at the very least enhancing the neural pathways involved in performing the practiced task. There are also benefits from ‘mental reps’, ie thinking through the steps of a physical action even if you don’t perform it. At a certain point actually performing a task leads to more measurable improvement than only mental reps, but mental reps still have inherent value.

Taking all that into consideration, there are a few obvious conclusions to draw in terms of how one should approach learning:

  • Positive thinking works (seen in Cognitive Behavioral Therapy). The act of thinking genuinely changes your neural pathways. Therefore thinking positively, and making positive associations with difficult workloads (ie thinking of something as a challenge rather than a burden) can lead to improved results.
  • Thinking about a task, visualization etc has genuine benefits to the learning process. This implies that there’s also value in immersion, even if too overwhelmed to get immediate benefit. This ties back into the value of positive thinking, and also being willing to chip away at a task.
  • Practice. Performing a task, even if only mentally, has measurable improvements in how you would perform that task in future. This should be apparent - think back to primary school when learning to count, or do arithmetic. Repetition leads to speed, accuracy, and confidence. Repetition leads to learning.
  • Translating a task into a more familiar shape can result in faster learning and completion rates. A relevant example is learning how to reshape a problem into a familiar problem - once you think of something as a graph problem, or a classic dynamic programming maximization it can be much easier to solve and understand.

In addition to these methods, there are ways to improve your neuroplasticity. They mostly boil down to getting enough rest, or varying the types of tasks you are doing (as well as introducing new ones), but for the sake of completeness, here are a few selected examples:

  • Get adequate sleep, and consider taking a nap. It’s no surprise that getting enough rest sets you up for a more productive day, and there is physical evidence that naps can encourage growth of Dendritic spines, tiny structures in your brain that can aid synaptic transmission and possibly even increase the number of possible connections between neurons. Rather technical for us poor laymen, but the message that napping can be useful ought to be clear.
  • Switching off. The brain needs rest just as much as it needs stimulation, and over-taxing the brain doesn’t result in improved outcomes.
  • Varying your tasks, and/or introducing new ones. There’s evidence that using less-used neural pathways in your motor cortex by using your non-dominant hand, balancing on one-leg, learning to juggle etc can have positive effects on your overall neuroplasticity. A similar process comes into play when learning a new language, listening (or playing) new music, practicing creativity with art.. thinking in new ways makes learning new things easier.

Growth Mindset

A growth mindset is, in simple-terms, a positive outlook/positive way of thinking that encourages embracing the idea of improvement, and rejecting the premise that your skills or abilities are entrenched or fixed. In understanding the concept of neuroplasticity, you must also accept that a growth mindset is desirable (and accurate) - we can literally physically change our own brains, which implies that our potentials are always much larger than we could imagine. To get the maximum benefit from neuroplasticity, we must first be open to the idea of growth, and willing to work towards it.

Neuroplasticity and the Growth Mindset were familiar concept to me when I started this course (and if they weren’t, we did cover it in the prep material for Dev Academy), but it’s valuable to be reminded of them from time-to-time, and make it a conscious process for at least a little while. I hope that I've already fully integrated the idea of growth into my learning, but it doesn’t hurt to take a step back and evaluate if you’re taking full advantage of it. I intend to continue to set small goals and achieve them, and keep looking at the more distant goals with a ‘not yet’ attitude.
